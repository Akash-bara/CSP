Food Adulteration

Definition

Food adulteration is the process of lowering the quality of food by adding, mixing, or substituting harmful or inferior substances, making it unsafe for consumption.

Types of Food Adulteration

  • Intentional – done for profit (e.g., adding water to milk).
  • Incidental – due to negligence (e.g., pesticide residues).
  • Metallic Contamination – from metals like lead, mercury, arsenic.
  • Packaging Hazards – toxins from non-food-grade materials.

Common Examples

  • Milk → water, starch, detergent.
  • Spices → brick powder, lead salts, artificial colors.
  • Oils → mixed with cheaper/argemone oil.
  • Vegetables/Fruits → ripened with carbide, coated with harmful wax.

Harmful Effects

  • Food poisoning, stomach disorders.
  • Damage to liver, kidney, heart.
  • Cancer, weak immunity.
  • Can cause death in severe cases.

Prevention / Control

  • Buy from trusted sources.
  • Look for FSSAI mark / food safety labels.
  • Wash fruits/vegetables thoroughly.
  • Avoid brightly/artificially colored foods.
  • Strong government regulations and consumer awareness.
